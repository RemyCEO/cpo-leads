# How to Land a Close Protection Job

**The no-bullshit guide from people who actually do this work.**

This isn't a motivational pamphlet. It's the playbook that would have saved you two years of wasted applications, three overpriced courses, and one very awkward LinkedIn message to a team leader who already forgot your name. Everything here comes from operators who've run details, staffed teams, and watched hundreds of candidates either make it or wash out.

---

## 1. Where the Jobs Actually Live (And Where They Don't)

Here's the thing nobody tells you at your SIA course graduation: the vast majority of close protection jobs never hit a public job board. They move through a parallel economy of private groups, word-of-mouth referrals, and recruiter shortlists that you won't find by typing "bodyguard jobs" into Indeed.

**The platforms that actually matter:**

- **Silent Professionals** — the gold standard for vetted CP/EP roles, particularly US-facing contracts. Military and law enforcement background required for most listings. Worth every dollar of the subscription.
- **Circuit Magazine** — the UK and international CP community's newspaper. Their job board is legitimate, and their network is how half the London circuit finds rotation work.
- **EP Wired** — US executive protection focused. Good for domestic UHNW and corporate details.
- **BBA (British Bodyguard Association)** — membership gets you access to their job alerts and, more importantly, their network events.
- **ISS World** (Intelligence Support Systems) — niche, but if you're looking at government-adjacent or surveillance-heavy roles, this is where those contracts surface.
- **ASIS International** — their career center skews corporate security, but Director of Protective Services roles and corporate EP programs post here.
- **IPSB** (International Professional Security Association) — smaller but legitimate, particularly for European contracts.

**Why Indeed and LinkedIn job posts are a graveyard:** By the time a CP job hits Indeed, it's either been filled internally, it's a bottom-feeder company paying day rates that wouldn't cover your fuel, or it's a recruiter harvesting CVs for a database they'll never actually use. LinkedIn job posts are marginally better, but the real LinkedIn value is in direct messaging — more on that in Section 3.

**The closed groups you need to find:** Telegram, WhatsApp, and Signal groups run by active operators are where the real-time tasking happens. "Need a body for Saturday, UHNW residential, £350/day" — that message goes out at 11pm Thursday and is filled by midnight. You won't find these groups advertised. You get added by someone who's worked with you, or someone who vouches for you. That's it. Ask operators you train with. Ask after courses. Be direct: "Are there any task groups I should be in?"

**Red flags for fake groups:** Any Telegram group with 5,000+ members advertising "VIP protection jobs in Dubai" with a link to a paid training course is a scam. Real task groups are small (50-200 people), moderated by a known operator, and nobody is selling you anything. If someone asks for money to join a WhatsApp group, walk away.

> **Anecdote:** A guy I know spent four months applying to CP jobs on Indeed. Sent 200+ applications. Got two replies — both from companies offering £120/day event work with no insurance. He then got added to one Telegram group by a bloke he'd done a HEAT course with. Had his first proper residential task within two weeks.

> **Common mistake:** Spending all your time on public job boards and wondering why nobody calls back. The jobs are there, but you're looking in the wrong room.

> **If I could only do one thing:** Get into two or three vetted Telegram/WhatsApp task groups. That single action will generate more work than six months of Indeed applications.

**Quick Win:** Sign up for Silent Professionals and Circuit Magazine this week. Set up alerts. Then ask every CP operator you know: "What task groups are you in, and can you put me forward?"

**Action bullets:**
- Subscribe to Silent Professionals, Circuit Magazine, and EP Wired today
- Join ASIS and BBA for network access, not just the job boards
- Ask three operators you know about closed task groups — be direct
- Delete your Indeed "bodyguard" saved search — it's giving you false hope
- Set a calendar reminder to check specialist boards every Monday and Thursday morning

## 2. The CV That Actually Gets Opened

Your CV has about eight seconds. That's not a metaphor — an ops manager with 40 CVs on his desk is scanning page one for three things: relevant experience, current certifications, and whether you sound like a professional or a Call of Duty enthusiast. If he doesn't find those in eight seconds, you're in the bin.

**Format and length:** Two pages. Never more. Page one is your shop window: name, contact details, a two-line professional summary, your last three relevant roles, and your certifications. Page two is everything else — earlier career, training courses, languages, references. Use a clean, simple layout. No photos of you in tactical gear. No logos. No colored backgrounds. PDF only, never Word.

**Military background — without sounding like everyone else:** Every second CV starts with "Former Royal Marines Commando with 12 years of operational experience." That's fine, but it doesn't differentiate you. Instead, translate military experience into principal-protection language. Don't say "conducted VCP operations in Helmand." Say "delivered secure movement planning and route advance in hostile environments." The ops manager knows what you mean. The HR person who screens before him doesn't speak military.

**Certifications that actually move the needle:**
- **SIA CP License** — non-negotiable for UK work. Without it, you don't exist.
- **FPOS-I** (First Person On Scene Intermediate) — minimum medical. FREC 3 or above is better.
- **MIRA** (Medical Instructor & Risk Assessment) or equivalent tactical medicine — separates you from the crowd.
- **HEAT** (Hostile Environment Awareness Training) — required for any conflict zone or high-risk rotation.
- **SSAFE** (Safe and Secure Approaches in Field Environments) — UN/NGO requirement.
- **Advanced Driving** — Tony Scotti's Vehicle Dynamics Institute or BSR (Bill Scott Raceway). "I can do J-turns" is not advanced driving. A course certificate from a recognized school is.
- **ASIS CPP/PSP** — corporate EP world values these. If you want corporate principal protection in the US, PSP (Physical Security Professional) shows you understand the ecosystem.
- **FAA Part 107** — drone pilot certification. Increasingly requested for advance work and estate security.

**Languages — be honest:** "Conversational French" means you can order a meal and ask for directions, not that you can coordinate with local police during an evacuation. List your languages with honest CEFR levels. Ops managers who've worked internationally can smell exaggeration instantly. Arabic, Mandarin, Portuguese, and French are the highest-value languages right now. Even basic proficiency is worth listing if you're honest about the level.

**Red flags that get your CV binned:**
- Tactical gear photos or "operator" selfies anywhere near your professional materials
- The word "operator" used unironically to describe yourself
- Claiming units or deployments you weren't part of. The CP world is tiny. Someone will check. Someone always checks.
- A three-page CV with your secondary school listed
- An email address like tacticalwarrior87@gmail.com

**References:** Three professional references, at least two from CP or security. Always ask permission before listing someone. Tell them what kind of role you're applying for so they're prepared when the call comes. A team leader you've worked under recently is worth more than a colonel who commanded your battalion a decade ago.

> **Anecdote:** An ops manager told me he once received a CV with a full-page photo of the applicant in plate carrier and night vision goggles. The guy had good experience underneath all that — genuinely solid background. But the photo told the ops manager everything he needed to know about judgment and discretion. Straight in the bin.

> **Common mistake:** Listing every course you've ever attended, including your 2009 manual handling certificate. Only list what's current and relevant to CP work.

> **If I could only do one thing:** Rewrite your professional summary to speak the principal's language, not the military's language. One paragraph that says "I keep people safe, I'm discreet, and I solve problems before they become incidents."

**Quick Win:** Open your CV right now. Delete everything from page three onwards. Move your certifications to page one. Remove any photo that isn't a headshot in a suit.

**Action bullets:**
- Two pages maximum, PDF format, clean layout, no tactical imagery
- Page one: summary, last three roles, current certs — this is your eight-second pitch
- Translate military jargon into protection language a civilian principal would understand
- List languages with honest CEFR levels — B2 means B2, not "I watched a Netflix series in Spanish"
- Get three references lined up and briefed before you start applying

## 3. The Cold Outreach Playbook

Most people in this industry are terrible at outreach. They send a CV to info@securitycompany.com with "Job Application" as the subject line and wonder why they never hear back. That email went straight to a shared inbox that nobody checks, was opened by an office administrator who doesn't know what FPOS-I means, and was filed under "misc."

Cold outreach works when you do the homework first.

**Research before you reach out:** Before you contact any company, spend 20 minutes understanding what they actually do. Who are their clients? UHNW families? Corporate executives? Touring entertainment? Maritime? Government contracts? This matters because your message needs to show you understand their world, not just that you exist and want a job.

**Finding the right person:** Don't email info@. Find the person who makes hiring decisions:
- **Ops Manager** — the person who actually staffs tasks. This is your primary target.
- **Head of Recruitment** — larger companies have these. They're gatekeepers but responsive ones.
- **Director of Protective Services** — corporate EP programs. Often the decision-maker and the person you'd report to.

Use LinkedIn to identify these people. Company websites sometimes list leadership. Circuit Magazine profiles occasionally name them. If you can't find a name, call the company's main line and ask: "Who handles recruitment for your close protection division?"

**Email structure that gets replies:**

Subject line: "CP Operative — [Your Key Differentiator] — Available [Month]"

Body — three lines max before your soft close:
1. One sentence showing you know what they do. "I see [Company] is expanding its UHNW residential coverage in the Gulf — that aligns directly with my recent experience."
2. One sentence on what you bring. "I've completed two rotations on a family office detail in Riyadh, hold current FPOS-I and SIA CP, and speak conversational Arabic."
3. Soft close. "I'd welcome the chance to introduce myself properly. Happy to send my CV or jump on a brief call at your convenience."

Attach your CV as a PDF. Don't paste it into the email body.

**Follow-up cadence:** Three touches over three weeks. First email. Then a brief follow-up at day seven: "Just bumping this to the top of your inbox — appreciate you're busy." Final touch at day 21, adding one new piece of information (a new certification, a recent task completion, a referral name). After three with no reply, move on. Do not send a fourth.

**LinkedIn DM strategy:** Connect first with a short note: "Hi [Name], I'm a CP operative looking to connect with professionals at [Company]. No pitch — just building my network in the industry." Once connected, wait 48 hours, then send a brief message similar to your email structure. LinkedIn DMs work better than email for some ops managers who live on the platform.

**WhatsApp/Signal:** Only appropriate if you've been given someone's number directly, or if you're already in a shared group. Never cold-message someone on WhatsApp. It feels invasive and marks you as someone with poor judgment about boundaries — the exact opposite of what a CP operative should project.

> **Anecdote:** One operator I know landed a permanent rotation with a Gulf-based family office by sending exactly three emails over three weeks to the ops manager. The first was ignored. The second got a one-line reply: "Send CV." The third, after sending the CV, was a note saying he'd just completed a MIRA refresher. He was on a plane within a month. Persistence without being annoying.

> **Common mistake:** Sending the same generic email to 50 companies. Ops managers talk to each other. If three of them received your identical "Dear Sir/Madam" email, they'll mention it — and not favorably.

> **If I could only do one thing:** Send five personalized emails this week to ops managers at companies whose work you genuinely understand and want to be part of.

**Quick Win:** Pick three companies you'd actually want to work for. Find the ops manager on LinkedIn. Send a connection request with a one-line note today.

**Action bullets:**
- Research every company before you contact them — 20 minutes minimum
- Find the ops manager or head of recruitment by name, never email info@
- Three-line email: show you know them, state your value, soft close
- Follow up three times over three weeks, then stop
- LinkedIn DMs are fair game after connecting; WhatsApp cold messages are not

## 4. The Recruiter Game

Recruiters in close protection fall into two categories: those who actually staff CP roles, and those who harvest your CV to fill a database they use to impress clients. Learning the difference saves you months.

**Family office and estate recruiters:** These are recruitment agencies that staff for ultra-high-net-worth families, estates, and private households. They recruit for roles that blend CP with estate management, driving, and sometimes travel coordination. The principal often wants someone who's discreet, presentable, and can switch between security awareness and carrying shopping bags without ego.

Key agencies:
- **Greycoat Lumleys** — London-based, strong in UHNW household staffing. They understand CP roles within a private staff structure.
- **Tiger Recruitment** — another London household agency. Their security roles lean toward residential and family protection.
- **Polo & Tweed** — estate and household recruitment with CP-adjacent positions.
- **Bespoke Bureau** — smaller but well-connected in the family office world.
- **Mahler Private Staffing** — European and Middle Eastern private household roles.

Register with all of them. Be clear about what you do and don't do. If you're not willing to do school runs or walk the dog, say so upfront — but understand that flexibility gets you through the door of some very lucrative long-term positions.

**CP-specific recruiters:** Companies like Mobius International, Pilgrims Group, and others that specialize in protective services have their own recruitment pipelines. Register formally through their websites, but then follow up with a direct email to the recruitment or ops team. Your registration form goes into a database. Your email to a named person gets you remembered.

**The availability email:** This is your most powerful tool for staying on a recruiter's radar. Once a month, on the first Monday, send a brief email to every recruiter and ops manager you've registered with:

Subject: "Availability Update — [Your Name] — [Month Year]"

Body:
"Hi [Name], quick update on my availability for [Month]. I'm available from [dates]. Recent updates: [one line — new cert, completed task, etc.]. Current location: [city/country]. Happy to discuss any upcoming requirements. Best, [Name] [Phone]"

That's it. No essay. No desperation. Just a professional reminder that you exist and you're ready. The operators who do this consistently are the ones who get the call when a last-minute slot opens.

**Red flags — recruiters to avoid:**
- Any recruiter who charges you an upfront fee. Legitimate recruitment agencies are paid by the client, never the candidate. No exceptions.
- "You need to complete our training course first." This is a training company disguised as a recruiter. They make money from selling courses, not from placing you. If your certifications are current and recognized, you don't need their proprietary two-day "executive protection masterclass."
- Recruiters who can't name a single client or recent placement. If they're vague about who they work for, they're probably not working for anyone.
- Agencies that promise guaranteed placement. Nobody can guarantee you a job in this industry. Anyone who says otherwise is selling you something.

> **Anecdote:** A mate registered with a well-known agency that told him he needed to complete their £2,500 "advanced EP assessment" before they could put him forward for roles. He had 15 years of military service and a current SIA license. He walked. Two months later he was placed by Greycoat Lumleys with no assessment, no fee, and a role that paid three times what the first agency was advertising.

> **Common mistake:** Registering with five agencies and then going silent. Recruiters fill roles fast. If you haven't reminded them you exist in the last 30 days, you're not on their mental shortlist when the call comes in at 4pm on a Friday.

> **If I could only do one thing:** Set up a monthly availability email template and send it to every recruiter on the first Monday of every month without fail.

**Quick Win:** Register with Greycoat Lumleys and two CP-specific recruiters this week. Send your first availability email on the next first Monday.

**Action bullets:**
- Register with family office recruiters (Greycoat Lumleys, Tiger, Polo & Tweed, Bespoke Bureau, Mahler)
- Register with CP-specific companies and follow up with a direct email to the ops team
- Send a monthly availability email on the first Monday — make it a non-negotiable calendar event
- Never pay an upfront fee to a recruiter — legitimate agencies are paid by clients
- If a recruiter says you need their training course first, walk away immediately

## 5. Networking That Actually Generates Work

You've heard it a thousand times: "It's not what you know, it's who you know." In close protection, this isn't just true — it's the entire operating model. Roughly 80% of CP work comes through personal referrals and people you've physically worked alongside. That means your network isn't a nice-to-have. It's your pipeline.

**Events worth attending:**
- **Security & Counter Terror Expo (SCTX)** — London, annual. The UK industry's main gathering. Good for meeting company reps and fellow operators.
- **ISC East and ISC West** — US-focused security expos. More corporate and tech-heavy, but corporate EP decision-makers attend.
- **Intersec Dubai** — if you're targeting Gulf contracts, this is mandatory. The networking floor is where relationships start that lead to rotation work.
- **Milipol Paris** — international security and defense. Government and diplomatic protection contacts.

These events aren't about collecting brochures. They're about putting a face to a name and having a three-minute conversation that makes someone remember you when a slot opens.

**Closed events that matter more:**
- **BBA dinners** — small, members-only. This is where senior operators and company directors actually talk shop.
- **ASIS chapter meetings** — regional, regular, and attended by corporate security directors who hire EP teams.
- **Circuit Magazine meetups** — informal but valuable. The Circuit community is tight, and showing up consistently builds recognition.

**The 80/20 rule in practice:** Four out of five jobs you'll ever work come from someone you've worked alongside saying "I know a bloke" when asked for a recommendation. This means every task you work is also a networking opportunity. Be professional. Be reliable. Be the person your team leader wants to call back.

**The referral script:** When you've built a relationship with someone, it's perfectly acceptable to say: "If anything comes up that you think I'd be a fit for, I'd really appreciate you putting my name forward. I'm available from [date] and my CV is current." Direct. No groveling. No "I'll do anything." Just a clear ask.

**Why the bar after conference matters more than the conference:** The panels and keynotes are fine. But the real business happens at the hotel bar at 9pm, or at the dinner table where six operators are swapping stories. That's where guard is down, where people talk about what they're actually working on, and where the "we need someone for next month" conversations happen. Show up. Buy a round. Listen more than you talk. Don't get drunk — you're still being assessed.

> **Anecdote:** An operator attended Intersec Dubai three years in a row. Didn't get a single job from it directly. But on the third year, a guy he'd had drinks with twice remembered him when his team needed a replacement for a Saudi residential detail. That single connection led to 18 months of continuous work and two further referrals. Networking is a long game.

> **Common mistake:** Treating networking events like job fairs — handing out CVs to everyone and asking "are you hiring?" within the first minute. Nobody wants to be pitched at a social event. Build the relationship first. The work follows.

> **If I could only do one thing:** Attend one industry event per quarter and have three genuine conversations at each. Not pitches. Conversations.

**Quick Win:** Look up the next BBA dinner, ASIS chapter meeting, or Circuit Magazine event in your area. Register today.

**Action bullets:**
- Attend at least one industry event per quarter — SCTX, Intersec, ASIS chapters, BBA dinners
- At every event, aim for three genuine conversations, not three CV handouts
- Stay for the bar. The real networking happens after the formal agenda ends
- After every task, tell your team leader you're available and ask them to keep you in mind
- Follow up with every new contact within 48 hours — a brief LinkedIn message or email

## 6. Building a Brand That Brings Work to You

Let's be clear about something: in close protection, "building a brand" does not mean what Instagram influencers think it means. You're not creating content. You're not building a following. You're creating a minimal, professional presence that confirms you're legitimate when someone Googles your name after receiving your CV.

That's it. Confirmation, not promotion.

**LinkedIn done right:** Your LinkedIn profile should look like a senior professional's, not a special forces recruitment poster.

- **Headline:** "Close Protection Operative | SIA Licensed | FPOS-I" — not "Elite Operator | Tier 1 Protector | Keeping Principals Safe Worldwide"
- **Photo:** Suit. Clean background. Professional headshot. Not you in a plate carrier. Not you standing next to a blacked-out SUV.
- **Summary:** Four to five sentences. What you do, where you've worked (regions, not client names), your certifications, your availability. Never name principals. Never post operational details. Ever.
- **Experience:** List roles with company names and date ranges. Brief descriptions of scope (residential, travel, event). No client names, no locations of safe houses, no details about security arrangements.

**Personal website — when it helps:** A simple one-page site with your name, professional summary, certifications, and a contact form can be useful if you're positioning yourself as a senior operator or consultant. It shows professionalism and makes you easy to find. Keep it clean, minimal, and free of stock photos of men in suits talking into earpieces.

When it hurts: if it looks like it was built in 2011, has a visitor counter, or features you posing with firearms. In that case, no website is better than a bad website.

**Circuit Magazine contributions:** Writing a short article or contributing to a discussion on Circuit shows you think about the profession, not just work in it. A well-written 800-word piece on advance work methodology, medical response in residential settings, or lessons from a specific operating environment positions you as someone with depth. Ops managers read Circuit. This is how you get noticed by people you've never met.

**The "boring is professional" principle:** The best close protection operatives are invisible. Your online presence should reflect that. No action shots. No cryptic Instagram posts about "another day at the office" with a blacked-out location. No TikToks from the motorcade. The operators who work the highest-level details are the ones you can't find online at all — or, if you can, the only thing you find is a clean LinkedIn profile and a professional headshot. Boring is the brand.

> **Anecdote:** An operator had a fantastic CV and great references, but when the ops manager Googled him, the first result was an Instagram account full of photos from details — blurred-out principals, identifiable vehicles, locations tagged. He didn't get the call. The ops manager later said: "If he posts that from my client's detail, I'm the one who gets fired."

> **Common mistake:** Over-sharing on social media. Even vague posts about "working abroad" or "long rotation" can compromise operational security. Principals and their staff Google you. Their lawyers Google you. Act accordingly.

> **If I could only do one thing:** Audit your entire online presence. Google your own name. Delete anything that looks unprofessional, operational, or tactical. Make your LinkedIn clean and minimal.

**Quick Win:** Google your own name right now. Fix or delete the first thing that an ops manager would raise an eyebrow at.

**Action bullets:**
- LinkedIn: professional headshot in a suit, clean headline, no operational details, no client names
- Delete or private any social media content showing tactical gear, operational environments, or identifiable details
- Consider a simple one-page personal website if you're positioning as a senior operator
- Write one article for Circuit Magazine or a similar industry publication this quarter
- Remember: the goal is to be findable and confirmable, not famous

## 7. Geography and Market Intelligence

Where you're willing to work determines what you earn, how often you work, and what kind of life you lead. The CP market isn't one market — it's dozens of micro-markets with wildly different dynamics, rates, and entry requirements. Knowing where the money is right now, versus where it was three years ago, is the difference between a full calendar and six months of silence.

**Where the money is in 2025-2026:**

- **UAE (Dubai, Abu Dhabi)** — still strong for UHNW residential and family protection. Day rates £400-600 for experienced operators. Visa sponsorship through security companies is common. Tax-free income.
- **Saudi Arabia (NEOM, Red Sea, Riyadh)** — the biggest growth market in CP right now. Mega-projects need security infrastructure from scratch. Rotation contracts (28/28 or 42/21) with day rates of £450-700. Challenging operating environment but the money is real.
- **US Executive Protection** — corporate EP programs for Fortune 500 executives are expanding. Rates vary wildly: $400-800/day depending on city and principal. Requires right to work in the US or specific visa arrangements.
- **London Family Office** — steady demand for discreet residential protection. £350-500/day. Requires SIA CP, immaculate presentation, and the ability to blend into a Mayfair household without looking like you're guarding a nightclub.

**Saturated markets to approach carefully:**
- **UK domestic event security** — oversupplied with SIA license holders. Day rates have been pushed down to £180-250. This is survival money, not career money.
- **Generic "VIP security" in party destinations** — Ibiza, Marbella, and similar. Low rates, unreliable clients, and attracts the worst end of the industry.

**Visa and work permit realities:**
- **UAE:** Employment visa through a sponsoring company. Relatively straightforward but tied to your employer.
- **Saudi Arabia:** Work visa through the contracting company. Longer processing, stricter requirements, but companies handle the logistics.
- **United States:** The hardest market to enter as a foreign national. H-1B is rarely used for CP. Most international operators work US details through companies that arrange O or P visas for specialized roles, or they're already permanent residents.
- **EU/Schengen:** Post-Brexit, UK nationals need work permits for EU countries. Some companies sponsor; many prefer EU passport holders for simplicity.

**Tax residency for rotation workers:** If you're on a rotation contract (28/28 or similar), your tax residency depends on where you spend the majority of your non-working days. Get proper tax advice before signing a rotation contract. A £500 consultation with an expat tax specialist saves you £20,000 in unexpected tax bills. This is not an area for guesswork.

**Day rates by market (2026 benchmarks — experienced operator, 5+ years):**
- London residential: £350-500/day
- UK events/touring: £250-400/day
- Dubai/UAE residential: £400-600/day
- Saudi rotation: £450-700/day
- US corporate EP: $500-900/day
- Hostile environment (Africa, MENA conflict zones): £500-800/day + danger pay
- Maritime (anti-piracy): £350-500/day + vessel rates

These are rates for operators with a solid track record and relevant certifications. If you're just starting, expect 20-30% less until you've built your reputation.

> **Anecdote:** An operator I know chased UK event work for two years, averaging £220/day and working maybe 15 days a month. He invested in a HEAT course, got a MIRA refresher, and applied for Saudi rotation contracts. Within four months he was on a 42/21 rotation at £550/day. Same skillset. Different geography. Three times the income.

> **Common mistake:** Staying in a saturated local market because it's comfortable. The best-paid CP work requires travel and flexibility. If you're only willing to work within an hour of your house, your options and rates shrink dramatically.

> **If I could only do one thing:** Research three companies operating in the Gulf right now and send them your CV this week. That's where the growth is.

**Quick Win:** Check the current job listings on Silent Professionals and Circuit Magazine. Note which regions appear most frequently. That tells you where demand is today, not where it was when your instructor was working.

**Action bullets:**
- Study the current demand map: Gulf, Saudi mega-projects, US corporate EP, London family office
- Get your passport, certifications, and medical documentation ready for international deployment
- Budget for a tax consultation if you're considering rotation contracts — do this before you sign
- Avoid racing to the bottom in saturated markets; invest in training that opens higher-rate geographies
- Track day rates across markets quarterly — rates shift, and last year's numbers are already outdated

## 8. The Interview and Vetting Stage

If your CV got you through the door, the interview is where you either confirm you're the professional they hoped for, or reveal that your CV was doing some heavy lifting. In close protection, interviews range from a casual phone call with an ops manager to a multi-stage process involving background checks, medical assessments, fitness tests, and a face-to-face with the principal.

**Background checks and timelines:** Expect DBS (Disclosure and Barring Service) enhanced checks in the UK, or equivalent criminal background checks in other jurisdictions. For government-adjacent contracts, expect developed vetting (DV) or security clearance (SC) processes that can take three to six months. Start these early if you can — some companies will fast-track candidates who already hold clearance.

Employment history verification is standard. They'll check your last five years minimum. Gaps in employment aren't necessarily a problem, but unexplained gaps are. If you took six months to travel or retrain, say so.

**Polygraph:** Yes, it exists in this industry. Some US government contracts, certain Gulf state positions, and a few corporate programs require polygraph examinations. They're testing for dishonesty on your application, substance abuse, and financial vulnerability. You can't prepare for a polygraph other than by telling the truth. That's the point.

**Medical and fitness:** Most serious companies require a current medical certificate. Some require specific fitness standards — typically a timed run, press-ups, sit-ups, and a basic strength assessment. Nothing extreme, but you should be able to pass a military-style basic fitness test without embarrassing yourself. If you can't run 2.4km in under 11 minutes, fix that before you apply.

**The principal interview:** This is the one that actually matters. Some principals never meet their security team directly. Others want to look you in the eye before they trust you around their family. The principal interview isn't really about your qualifications — those were verified before you got to this point. It's about:

- Do they feel comfortable around you?
- Can you carry a conversation without being awkward or oversharing?
- Do you look presentable? (Suit. Clean. Groomed. No tactical watch the size of a dinner plate.)
- Can you read social cues and adjust your behavior to the environment?
- Do you listen more than you talk?

Dress appropriately for the principal's world, not yours. If you're meeting a tech CEO in Silicon Valley, a navy suit without a tie might be right. If it's a Gulf royal family, a formal dark suit with a conservative tie. Ask the ops manager what to expect.

**Questions to ask them:** You're being interviewed, but you're also evaluating them. Questions that show professionalism:
- "What does the current team structure look like, and where would I fit in?"
- "What's the rotation schedule, and how is downtime managed?"
- "Are there specific training requirements you'd want me to complete?"
- "What's the protocol for incident reporting and escalation?"
- "How do you handle kit procurement — personal or company-issued?"

Do not ask about salary first. Let them bring it up, or raise it naturally near the end after you've established value.

**Salary negotiation:** Know the market rate for the specific role, region, and rotation before you walk in. Use the benchmarks from Section 7 as a starting point. State your expectation clearly: "Based on the role scope and my experience, I'd be looking at £X per day." Then stop talking. Don't justify, don't apologize, don't undercut yourself. If they counter, you can negotiate, but never drop more than 10% from your initial number — it signals desperation.

> **Anecdote:** An operator was brought in for a principal interview with a London-based family. He wore a tactical-style watch, boots instead of dress shoes, and kept his hands clasped behind his back like he was standing at ease. The principal's wife later told the ops manager: "He seemed like he was guarding a prison, not our home." He wasn't called back. The job went to a guy with less experience but who showed up in a tailored suit and had an easy conversation about the family's travel schedule.

> **Common mistake:** Treating the principal interview like a military briefing. Principals want to feel safe, not intimidated. Relax your posture. Make eye contact. Smile when appropriate. You're joining their household, not their fire team.

> **If I could only do one thing:** Practice a 60-second introduction that covers who you are, what you do, and why you're a fit — in plain English, not military jargon. Say it out loud until it sounds natural.

**Quick Win:** Get a current medical certificate and a fitness test done this month, before you need them. Having these ready removes a common delay in the hiring process.

**Action bullets:**
- Start DBS/security clearance processes early — they take time and can't be rushed
- Maintain baseline fitness: 2.4km in under 11 minutes, basic strength, no excuses
- For principal interviews: suit, groomed, relaxed, conversational — not tactical
- Prepare three questions to ask them that show you think about the role professionally
- Know your rate before you walk in and state it without apologizing

## 9. First Contracts and Staying Hired

Landing the first contract is hard. Staying hired is harder. The first 90 days on any detail determine whether you become part of the furniture or get replaced at the next rotation change. In an industry built on reputation, your first contracts aren't just jobs — they're auditions for every job that comes after.

**The first 90 days rule:** Everything you do in your first three months is being watched. Not just by the team leader — by the entire team, by household staff, by the principal's PA, and often by the principal themselves. They're evaluating:

- Do you show up early and prepared?
- Do you follow SOPs without being told twice?
- Are you proactive about identifying gaps in the security plan?
- Can you handle boredom without losing focus? (Most CP work is 95% waiting and 5% action. The waiting is the test.)
- Do you integrate with the team, or do you create friction?

The operators who get invited back are the ones who make everyone's job easier. They're the ones who noticed the gate sensor was playing up and reported it before anyone asked. Who learned the principal's schedule preferences without being briefed three times. Who got along with the housekeeper, the driver, and the nanny — because those people talk to the principal too.

**The "quiet professional" reputation:** This phrase gets thrown around a lot, but it means something specific. It means you do excellent work and don't talk about it. You don't post about it. You don't hint about it at dinner parties. You don't namedrop your principal to impress other operators. The moment you start trading war stories about current details, you become a liability.

The best reputation in this industry is this: "He's solid. He'll handle it. No drama." That's it. That's the whole brand. Be the person other operators describe that way.

**How principals communicate referrals:** Most UHNW principals don't write LinkedIn recommendations. They tell their friends. "Our security is excellent — I'll have my PA send you the details." That single sentence, spoken at a dinner party you'll never attend, is worth more than a hundred LinkedIn endorsements. It happens because you were professional, discreet, and made their life feel safer without making it feel restricted.

Their PA then calls your ops manager. Your ops manager calls you. "There's a new family who needs someone. Your name came up." That's how the top end of this industry works. You never see the referral happen. You just get the call.

**Avoiding the bottom-of-market trap:** Once you accept low-rate event work, you get typecast. Ops managers who pay £200/day for festival security don't suddenly offer you a £500/day residential rotation. You become a "festival guy" or a "nightclub guy." If you need to take lower-rate work to pay bills — and everyone does at some point — do it, but keep investing in the training and networking that moves you into higher-rate environments. Don't let temporary survival work define your career trajectory.

> **Anecdote:** An operator on his first residential detail noticed that the family's children's school route had a blind corner with poor sightlines and no alternate approach. He mapped an alternative route, drove it twice, documented it, and presented it to the team leader without being asked. The team leader mentioned it to the principal. That operator has been with the same family for four years and has been promoted to team leader himself. One proactive action. Four years of continuous employment.

> **Common mistake:** Treating a contract as a job rather than an audition. Every detail you work is an interview for the next one. Phone it in on a "boring" residential gig, and you won't get called for the interesting ones.

> **If I could only do one thing:** In your first week on any new detail, identify one security improvement and present it professionally to your team leader. Not to show off — to show you're paying attention.

**Quick Win:** On your next task, learn the names and roles of every household staff member within the first 48 hours. Treat them with respect. They have the principal's ear more than you do.

**Action bullets:**
- Treat the first 90 days as an extended interview — everything is being observed
- Be proactive: identify improvements and present them without being asked
- Build relationships with household staff — they influence whether you stay
- Never discuss current details with anyone outside the team. Ever.
- Avoid getting trapped in low-rate work by continuing to train and network into higher markets

## 10. Honest Realities Most Won't Tell You

This section won't make anyone feel good. But it's the section that would have saved a lot of operators a lot of wasted time, money, and frustration if someone had told them on day one.

**The industry is small. Dangerously small.** Burn a bridge with one company, and the ops manager at the next company already knows about it. This is an industry where the same 200 people keep running into each other across three continents. Word travels. If you leave a contract badly — walking off a task, getting into conflicts with team members, breaching confidentiality — that follows you. Not forever, but for years. And in those years, the work goes to someone else.

This isn't just about being fired. Even legitimate professional disagreements need to be handled carefully. If you have an issue with an ops manager, raise it professionally, privately, and factually. Don't vent on WhatsApp groups. Don't post about it. Don't trash the company in front of other operators. Handle it like the professional you claim to be.

**Nepotism is real. Work with it.** The son of the ops manager gets the first call. The team leader's training buddy gets the next rotation. The guy who went to Sandhurst with the company director gets the interview. This is how it works. You can rage against it, or you can build your own network of people who'll put your name forward when their son's not available. Eventually, you become the guy doing the recommending. But only if you've been professional and reliable long enough for people to stake their own reputation on yours.

**Dry seasons are normal.** There will be months — sometimes three, sometimes six — where the phone doesn't ring. No tasks. No rotation offers. Nothing. This is not a sign that your career is over. It's a feature of an industry built on short-term contracts and seasonal demand. Every experienced operator has been through it.

This is why financial planning matters. Maintain a minimum six-month runway of living expenses at all times. When work is flowing, save aggressively. Don't upgrade your lifestyle to match your best months, because your worst months are coming. They always do.

**Insurance, kit, and training come from your pocket.** Unless you're on a permanent salaried position (rare), you're responsible for:
- Professional indemnity insurance
- Your own medical kit and first-line equipment
- Ongoing training and certification renewals
- Travel costs to and from task locations (sometimes)
- Appropriate clothing (suits, formal wear, climate-appropriate gear)

Budget £3,000-5,000 per year for training and certification maintenance. Budget £1,000-2,000 for kit and clothing. Budget for insurance. These are the costs of being a professional, and nobody reimburses them when you're between contracts.

**Mental health and decompression.** This is the one nobody wants to talk about. Rotation work means long periods away from family and normal life, followed by sudden returns to a reality that moved on without you. Residential details can mean months of hypervigilance with no incident — which is success, but your nervous system doesn't know that. Hostile environment work carries obvious psychological weight.

Decompression is not a luxury. It's maintenance. Find what works for you — exercise, therapy, time outdoors, structured downtime — and do it consistently. The operators who burn out aren't usually the ones who faced the worst situations. They're the ones who never processed the normal ones.

If you're struggling, Samaritans (116 123 in the UK), the Veterans' Mental Health Crisis Line (0800 138 1619), and organizations like Combat Stress and PTSD Resolution offer confidential support. Using them doesn't make you weak. It makes you a professional who maintains their equipment — including the equipment between their ears.

> **Anecdote:** A very experienced operator — 20 years, top-tier clients, excellent reputation — told me he'd gone through an eight-month dry spell after a major client ended their contract. He almost left the industry. Then three calls came in the same week, and he was back on rotation within a month. He said the dry spell taught him more about financial planning than any course he'd ever taken. Now he keeps 12 months of expenses saved, not six.

> **Common mistake:** Living large during the good months and panicking during the dry ones. This is a feast-or-famine industry. Treat it accordingly from day one.

> **If I could only do one thing:** Open a separate savings account today and set up an automatic transfer for 20% of every payment you receive. Build that six-month runway before you build anything else.

**Quick Win:** Check your insurance coverage right now. If you don't have professional indemnity and personal accident insurance, get quotes this week.

**Action bullets:**
- Protect your reputation — every interaction in this industry is permanent
- Accept that nepotism exists and build your own referral network instead of complaining about it
- Maintain a six-month financial runway minimum — twelve months is better
- Budget £3,000-5,000 annually for training, kit, and insurance
- Take mental health seriously: find your decompression method and use it consistently
